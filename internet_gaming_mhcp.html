🙋👐 For Patient & Caregiver
WHO IS THIS FOR?
This plan is for family caregivers (caring for loved ones with illness, disability, dementia, mental health conditions), professional caregivers, and helping professionals (healthcare workers, therapists, first responders, social workers, teachers, clergy, hospice workers). Caregiver burnout affects 40-70% of family caregivers. Compassion fatigue is a real condition — secondary trauma from caring for others. Caregivers have higher rates of depression, anxiety, physical illness, and earlier mortality. You cannot pour from an empty cup. Caring for yourself is part of caring for others. This is the foundation of sustainable caregiving.

🛡️ Crisis Safety Planning
🛡️ My Crisis Safety Plan
Fill this out NOW — when you are well — so it is ready when you need it. Based on the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention.
Caregivers have elevated suicide risk and are often hidden patients. Safety plan addresses recognizing your own crisis, accessing emergency respite, breaking isolation, and getting your own care.
Step 1 — My personal warning signs (thoughts, feelings, situations that come BEFORE a crisis)
Step 2 — Coping strategies I can do alone (without contacting anyone)
Step 3 — People who provide distraction (places I can go where I do not have to talk about my feelings)
Step 4 — People I can ask for help (3 trusted people with phone numbers)
Step 5 — Professionals I can contact
Step 6 — Making my environment safer (reduce access to means of harm)
Step 7 — My reasons for living (what matters most)
📌 Print this and keep it on your fridge, in your wallet, and saved on your phone. Share with your treatment team and trusted person.

🌟 Beyond the Basics — What Often Gets Missed
🌸 Trauma-Informed Approach
Understanding how past trauma affects current symptoms and healing
▼
Caregiving is often traumatic — witnessing suffering, multiple losses, life-threatening situations. Helping professionals develop secondary trauma from exposure to others' trauma. Trauma-informed self-care includes recognizing trauma response, processing in supervision/therapy, limiting trauma exposure when possible.
🕊️ Cultural & Faith Considerations
Mental health care that respects your culture, faith, and identity
▼
Cultural expectations of caregiving vary widely. Some cultures expect family caregiving as duty; resisting feels shameful. Some have strong community caregiving. Religious frameworks for caregiving can sustain or burden. Cultural humility from providers important.
💵 Insurance & Financial Navigation (USA)
Getting affordable care — Medicaid, Medicare, ACA, sliding scale clinics, free options
▼
Medicare covers some respite (especially through hospice). Medicaid waivers often cover home care/respite — varies by state. Veterans caregivers may qualify for VA benefits. Long-term care insurance may cover respite. FMLA covers caregiving (up to 12 weeks). Workplace EAP often covers therapy. Tax credits for some caregivers.
🌙 Sleep & Circadian Rhythm
Why sleep matters more than almost anything else — and how to fix it
▼
Caregiver sleep is often disrupted (overnight care, hypervigilance). Sleep deprivation worsens everything. Address sleep proactively even with caregiving demands.
🌱 Gut-Brain Connection & Nutrition
How food, microbiome, and inflammation affect mental health
▼
Caregivers often eat poorly, skip meals, drink excess caffeine. Affects mood and energy. Schedule meals. Stress affects gut.
🤝 Peer Support — Why It Matters & How to Find It
Connecting with others who get it — the underrated foundation of recovery
▼
Caregiver peer support is uniquely valuable — they understand. Caregiver Action Network, Family Caregiver Alliance, disease-specific groups (Alzheimer's Association, NAMI Family-to-Family, Caregivers of Mental Health). Online groups for those who can't leave home. Wellness Together for healthcare workers.
📜 Psychiatric Advance Directive (PAD)
Decide your care while well — to be honored if you cannot speak for yourself
▼
Get YOUR advance directives done too — easy to focus only on care recipient. Care recipient should have: healthcare proxy, durable POA, advance directive, will. You should have: backup caregiver plan, your own advance directives.

📚 References & Further Reading
The following peer-reviewed studies support the guidance in this care plan. Each includes a plain-English summary.
1. Schulz R, Sherwood PR (2008). Physical and mental health effects of family caregiving. Am J Nurs, 108(9 Suppl):23-27.
What this means for you: Caregivers have higher illness, depression, mortality.
2. Adelman RD et al (2014). Caregiver burden: a clinical review. JAMA, 311(10):1052-1060.
What this means for you: Comprehensive review of caregiver burden and interventions.
3. Cocker F, Joss N (2016). Compassion fatigue among healthcare and emergency service professionals. Int J Environ Res Public Health, 13(6):618.
What this means for you: Compassion fatigue is significant occupational issue.
4. Pinquart M, Sörensen S (2003). Differences between caregivers and noncaregivers in psychological health and physical health. Psychol Aging, 18(2):250-267.
What this means for you: Caregiving has measurable health effects.
5. Sorensen S et al (2002). How effective are interventions with caregivers?. Gerontologist, 42(3):356-372.
What this means for you: Multiple effective interventions exist for caregiver support.
⚠️ IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER
This care plan is for educational purposes only. It is NOT a substitute for professional mental health care, diagnosis, or treatment. Always work with a licensed mental health professional (psychiatrist, psychologist, therapist, social worker, or counselor) for diagnosis and personalized treatment planning.

If you are in crisis: Call or text 988 (USA Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) immediately. If you have an immediate safety emergency, call 911 (USA), 999 (UK), 000 (Australia), or 112 (Europe). You can also go to any hospital emergency room.

Mental health conditions are real medical conditions — not character flaws or weaknesses. Seeking help is a sign of strength. Recovery is possible. Most people with mental illness lead full, meaningful lives with proper treatment and support.

My Health Harbor Foundation is a nonprofit educational organization. We do not provide direct mental health care, prescribe medications, or replace your treatment team.
