🙋👐 For Patient & Caregiver
WHO IS THIS FOR?
This plan is for people grieving the death of a loved one and the family and friends supporting them. Grief is not a mental illness — it is the natural response to loss. But grief is also one of the most painful human experiences. There is no "right way" to grieve and no fixed timeline. Most acute grief slowly transforms over months and years; some people develop prolonged grief disorder (separate care plan available). Grief affects body, mind, relationships, and spirit. Cultural and religious practices around grief vary widely and matter. This plan supports normal grief and helps identify when more help is needed.

🛡️ Crisis Safety Planning
🛡️ My Crisis Safety Plan
Fill this out NOW — when you are well — so it is ready when you need it. Based on the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention.
Suicidal thoughts in grief are more common than known. Safety plan addresses suicidal thoughts in grief, isolation, anniversary dates, and accessing support during acute waves.
Step 1 — My personal warning signs (thoughts, feelings, situations that come BEFORE a crisis)
Step 2 — Coping strategies I can do alone (without contacting anyone)
Step 3 — People who provide distraction (places I can go where I do not have to talk about my feelings)
Step 4 — People I can ask for help (3 trusted people with phone numbers)
Step 5 — Professionals I can contact
Step 6 — Making my environment safer (reduce access to means of harm)
Step 7 — My reasons for living (what matters most)
📌 Print this and keep it on your fridge, in your wallet, and saved on your phone. Share with your treatment team and trusted person.

🌟 Beyond the Basics — What Often Gets Missed
🌸 Trauma-Informed Approach
Understanding how past trauma affects current symptoms and healing
▼
Some deaths are themselves traumatic — sudden death, violence, suicide, witnessing death. Trauma symptoms (flashbacks, hypervigilance, avoidance) can co-occur with grief. Trauma-focused therapy can be needed alongside grief support. EMDR and prolonged exposure work for trauma.
🕊️ Cultural & Faith Considerations
Mental health care that respects your culture, faith, and identity
▼
Cultural and religious practices around death and grief are profound and varied. Some traditions have specific mourning periods, rituals, taboos, and supports. Honor what is meaningful to you and your community. Cultural humility from providers matters. Find culturally congruent support when possible.
💵 Insurance & Financial Navigation (USA)
Getting affordable care — Medicaid, Medicare, ACA, sliding scale clinics, free options
▼
Bereavement counseling is often covered by insurance if billed under depression/anxiety. Hospice provides 13 months of free bereavement services if loved one was on hospice — even non-family members. EAP through work often covers grief sessions. Many grief support groups are free.
🌙 Sleep & Circadian Rhythm
Why sleep matters more than almost anything else — and how to fix it
▼
Grief disrupts sleep — both falling asleep (racing thoughts) and waking (early morning waking common in grief). Be gentle with yourself. Avoid sleep medications long-term. Sleep WILL eventually improve.
🌱 Gut-Brain Connection & Nutrition
How food, microbiome, and inflammation affect mental health
▼
Grief affects appetite — many lose appetite, some eat for comfort. Either is normal short-term. Try to eat regularly even small amounts. Hydration easy to forget. Body needs fuel to grieve.
🤝 Peer Support — Why It Matters & How to Find It
Connecting with others who get it — the underrated foundation of recovery
▼
Grief support groups are often the most helpful intervention. GriefShare (faith-based), The Compassionate Friends (loss of child), Soaring Spirits (loss of partner), The Dougy Center (children), AFSP healing conversations (suicide loss). Hospice bereavement groups often free.
📜 Psychiatric Advance Directive (PAD)
Decide your care while well — to be honored if you cannot speak for yourself
▼
Not directly applicable to grief, but loss of a loved one is often a time when people consider their own advance directives — end of life wishes, durable power of attorney, healthcare proxies. Important conversations.

📚 References & Further Reading
The following peer-reviewed studies support the guidance in this care plan. Each includes a plain-English summary.
1. Bonanno GA (2009). The Other Side of Sadness: What the New Science of Bereavement Tells Us. Basic Books.
What this means for you: Most people are resilient in grief — recovery is the norm.
2. Shear MK (2015). Complicated grief. N Engl J Med, 372(2):153-160.
What this means for you: Distinguishes normal acute grief from prolonged/complicated grief.
3. Stroebe MS, Schut H (1999). The dual process model of coping with bereavement. Death Stud, 23(3):197-224.
What this means for you: Healthy grieving alternates between loss-focused and restoration-focused activities.
4. Aoun SM et al (2018). Bereavement support for family caregivers. Palliat Med, 32(8):1378-1388.
What this means for you: Support reduces complicated grief outcomes.
5. Currier JM et al (2008). The effectiveness of psychotherapeutic interventions for bereaved persons. Psychol Bull, 134(5):648-661.
What this means for you: Therapy is helpful for those struggling with grief, less needed for normal grief.
⚠️ IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER
This care plan is for educational purposes only. It is NOT a substitute for professional mental health care, diagnosis, or treatment. Always work with a licensed mental health professional (psychiatrist, psychologist, therapist, social worker, or counselor) for diagnosis and personalized treatment planning.

If you are in crisis: Call or text 988 (USA Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) immediately. If you have an immediate safety emergency, call 911 (USA), 999 (UK), 000 (Australia), or 112 (Europe). You can also go to any hospital emergency room.

Mental health conditions are real medical conditions — not character flaws or weaknesses. Seeking help is a sign of strength. Recovery is possible. Most people with mental illness lead full, meaningful lives with proper treatment and support.

My Health Harbor Foundation is a nonprofit educational organization. We do not provide direct mental health care, prescribe medications, or replace your treatment team.
