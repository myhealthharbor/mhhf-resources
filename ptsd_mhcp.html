🙋👐 For Patient & Caregiver
WHO IS THIS FOR?
This plan is for people living with intellectual disability (ID) and their family members and caregivers. Intellectual disability involves limitations in intellectual functioning and adaptive behavior beginning in childhood, affecting about 1-3% of the population. People with ID have 3-4x higher rates of mental health conditions than the general population — but their mental health needs are often missed or attributed to "behavior." This is called diagnostic overshadowing. Anxiety, depression, trauma responses, and psychosis all occur in people with ID and are treatable. Communication may look different — distress often shows as behavior change. This plan supports both the person with ID and their caregivers.

🛡️ Crisis Safety Planning
🛡️ My Crisis Safety Plan
Fill this out NOW — when you are well — so it is ready when you need it. Based on the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention.
Safety planning for people with ID involves caregivers heavily. Plan addresses suicide risk (often missed in ID), self-injury, aggression, and abuse risk (people with ID experience abuse at very high rates).
Step 1 — My personal warning signs (thoughts, feelings, situations that come BEFORE a crisis)
Step 2 — Coping strategies I can do alone (without contacting anyone)
Step 3 — People who provide distraction (places I can go where I do not have to talk about my feelings)
Step 4 — People I can ask for help (3 trusted people with phone numbers)
Step 5 — Professionals I can contact
Step 6 — Making my environment safer (reduce access to means of harm)
Step 7 — My reasons for living (what matters most)
📌 Print this and keep it on your fridge, in your wallet, and saved on your phone. Share with your treatment team and trusted person.

🌟 Beyond the Basics — What Often Gets Missed
🌸 Trauma-Informed Approach
Understanding how past trauma affects current symptoms and healing
▼
People with intellectual disability experience abuse at extremely high rates — sexual, physical, emotional. Trauma often presents as behavior change. Trauma is treatable. Use trauma-informed approaches even when communication is limited.
🕊️ Cultural & Faith Considerations
Mental health care that respects your culture, faith, and identity
▼
Why this matters: Mental health is deeply shaped by culture, race, ethnicity, religion, language, gender identity, and sexual orientation. The "standard" approaches often miss what makes care meaningful for YOU.

Cultural considerations:
• Stigma: Many cultures still view mental illness as weakness, spiritual failing, or shameful. Your care plan can honor your culture WHILE getting you proper treatment.
• Family involvement: In many cultures, family is central to healing. Bring trusted family to appointments if helpful.
• Language: You have the right to a translator. Therapy in your first language is significantly more effective.
• Body-mind connection: Some cultures express distress as physical symptoms. Both are valid.

Faith & spirituality in mental health:
• Faith communities can provide profound support and meaning
• Pastoral counselors (trained clergy) can be part of your treatment team
• Prayer, meditation, scripture, ritual — all evidence-based as adjuncts to clinical care
• If your faith tradition discourages medication or therapy, find clergy who support both
• Some Christian, Muslim, Jewish, Hindu, and Buddhist therapists integrate faith with clinical practice

Find culturally-matched therapists:
• Therapy for Black Girls / Therapy for Black Men — therapyforblackgirls.com
• Therapy for Latinx — therapyforlatinx.com
• Asian Mental Health Collective — asianmhc.org
• National Queer & Trans Therapists of Color Network — nqttcn.com
• Inclusive Therapists — inclusivetherapists.com

💵 Insurance & Financial Navigation (USA)
Getting affordable care — Medicaid, Medicare, ACA, sliding scale clinics, free options
▼
Medicaid waivers often cover services for people with ID — varies by state. Contact state Department of Developmental Disabilities. SSI/SSDI for income support. Special Needs Trusts for inheritance planning. The Arc can help navigate.
🌙 Sleep & Circadian Rhythm
Why sleep matters more than almost anything else — and how to fix it
▼
Sleep issues very common in ID — often medical (sleep apnea, GI issues), medication side effects, or anxiety. Sleep deprivation worsens behavior and mental health. Investigate sleep issues.
🌱 Gut-Brain Connection & Nutrition
How food, microbiome, and inflammation affect mental health
▼
Constipation, GERD, and other GI issues very common in ID and often unrecognized. Cause behavior change. Address proactively.
🤝 Peer Support — Why It Matters & How to Find It
Connecting with others who get it — the underrated foundation of recovery
▼
Self-advocacy groups like Self Advocates Becoming Empowered (SABE) and People First. Special Olympics for activity and connection. Best Buddies for friendship. The Arc for family support and advocacy.
📜 Psychiatric Advance Directive (PAD)
Decide your care while well — to be honored if you cannot speak for yourself
▼
Supported decision-making is increasingly preferred over guardianship. Advance directives can include behavior support preferences, medication preferences, and crisis plans.

📚 References & Further Reading
The following peer-reviewed studies support the guidance in this care plan. Each includes a plain-English summary.
1. Cooper SA et al (2007). Mental ill-health in adults with intellectual disabilities. Br J Psychiatry, 190:27-35.
What this means for you: Mental health conditions affect 40%+ of adults with ID — and they are treatable.
2. Vereenooghe L, Langdon PE (2013). Psychological therapies for adults with intellectual disabilities. Res Dev Disabil, 34(11):4085-4102.
What this means for you: Talk therapies work when adapted for people with ID.
3. Mazza MG et al (2020). Prevalence of co-occurring psychiatric disorders in adults with ID. J Intellect Disabil Res, 64(7):524-555.
What this means for you: High rates of co-occurring mental health conditions in ID.
4. Lunsky Y et al (2018). Crisis services for people with IDD. J Appl Res Intellect Disabil.
What this means for you: Specialized crisis approaches improve outcomes.
5. Sheehan R et al (2015). Mental illness, challenging behaviour, and psychotropic drug prescribing. BMJ, 351:h4326.
What this means for you: Psychotropic overprescribing is a common problem to watch for.
⚠️ IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER
This care plan is for educational purposes only. It is NOT a substitute for professional mental health care, diagnosis, or treatment. Always work with a licensed mental health professional (psychiatrist, psychologist, therapist, social worker, or counselor) for diagnosis and personalized treatment planning.

If you are in crisis: Call or text 988 (USA Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) immediately. If you have an immediate safety emergency, call 911 (USA), 999 (UK), 000 (Australia), or 112 (Europe). You can also go to any hospital emergency room.

Mental health conditions are real medical conditions — not character flaws or weaknesses. Seeking help is a sign of strength. Recovery is possible. Most people with mental illness lead full, meaningful lives with proper treatment and support.

My Health Harbor Foundation is a nonprofit educational organization. We do not provide direct mental health care, prescribe medications, or replace your treatment team.
