🙋👐 For Patient & Caregiver
WHO IS THIS FOR?
This plan is for people experiencing mental health symptoms during pregnancy (antenatal/prenatal mental health), after pregnancy loss (miscarriage, stillbirth, termination), or navigating fertility challenges. Mental health conditions during pregnancy affect 1 in 5 pregnant people — more common than gestational diabetes. Untreated antenatal depression and anxiety affect both parent and developing baby. Treatment in pregnancy is possible and important. Pregnancy loss is common and traumatic — grief and depression are normal responses needing support. Birthing partners and non-birthing parents also experience perinatal mental health challenges.

🛡️ Crisis Safety Planning
🛡️ My Crisis Safety Plan
Fill this out NOW — when you are well — so it is ready when you need it. Based on the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention.
Safety planning in perinatal period addresses elevated suicide risk during pregnancy and postpartum, the impact of pregnancy loss, and birth trauma. Postpartum plan should be made BEFORE birth.
Step 1 — My personal warning signs (thoughts, feelings, situations that come BEFORE a crisis)
Step 2 — Coping strategies I can do alone (without contacting anyone)
Step 3 — People who provide distraction (places I can go where I do not have to talk about my feelings)
Step 4 — People I can ask for help (3 trusted people with phone numbers)
Step 5 — Professionals I can contact
Step 6 — Making my environment safer (reduce access to means of harm)
Step 7 — My reasons for living (what matters most)
📌 Print this and keep it on your fridge, in your wallet, and saved on your phone. Share with your treatment team and trusted person.

🌟 Beyond the Basics — What Often Gets Missed
🌸 Trauma-Informed Approach
Understanding how past trauma affects current symptoms and healing
▼
Pregnancy can activate previous trauma — especially sexual trauma, medical trauma, previous birth trauma. Trauma-informed prenatal and birth care reduces re-traumatization. Birth itself can be traumatic for 25-34% of birthing people. Pregnancy loss is traumatic and the grief is real.
🕊️ Cultural & Faith Considerations
Mental health care that respects your culture, faith, and identity
▼
Cultural traditions around pregnancy and loss vary widely. Some have strong rituals; some have silence. Pregnancy loss is often hidden in some cultures, increasing isolation. Find culturally congruent care when possible.
💵 Insurance & Financial Navigation (USA)
Getting affordable care — Medicaid, Medicare, ACA, sliding scale clinics, free options
▼
Pregnancy is qualifying event for insurance enrollment. Medicaid covers pregnancy in all states. Mental health is essential health benefit. Some states have specific perinatal Medicaid extensions. FMLA covers serious mental health conditions related to pregnancy.
🌙 Sleep & Circadian Rhythm
Why sleep matters more than almost anything else — and how to fix it
▼
Pregnancy disrupts sleep — especially third trimester. Plan sleep proactively. Postpartum sleep deprivation is medical issue, not weakness.
🌱 Gut-Brain Connection & Nutrition
How food, microbiome, and inflammation affect mental health
▼
Pregnancy affects gut significantly — nausea, constipation, reflux. Affects mental health. Eat regularly. Address symptoms.
🤝 Peer Support — Why It Matters & How to Find It
Connecting with others who get it — the underrated foundation of recovery
▼
Postpartum Support International includes pregnancy and loss support. Pregnancy after Loss Support. Star Legacy Foundation for stillbirth. Resolve for fertility. Share Pregnancy & Infant Loss Support. Peer mentors who have walked this path.
📜 Psychiatric Advance Directive (PAD)
Decide your care while well — to be honored if you cannot speak for yourself
▼
Birth plan documents preferences but should be flexible. Mental health crisis plan for postpartum made BEFORE birth. Advance directives for care if you become incapacitated.

📚 References & Further Reading
The following peer-reviewed studies support the guidance in this care plan. Each includes a plain-English summary.
1. Howard LM et al (2014). Non-psychotic mental disorders in the perinatal period. Lancet, 384(9956):1775-1788.
What this means for you: Mental health conditions affect 1 in 5 pregnant people — getting help matters.
2. Stein A et al (2014). Effects of perinatal mental disorders on the fetus and child. Lancet, 384(9956):1800-1819.
What this means for you: Untreated mental illness has effects too — treatment supports both you and baby.
3. Molenaar NM et al (2018). Guidelines on treatment of perinatal depression with antidepressants. J Affect Disord, 234:69-78.
What this means for you: Most SSRIs are compatible with pregnancy when treatment is needed.
4. Bauer A et al (2014). The costs of perinatal mental health problems. LSE Centre for Mental Health.
What this means for you: Untreated perinatal illness has major personal and societal costs.
5. Wenzel A et al (2005). Anxiety symptoms and disorders during pregnancy. J Anxiety Disord, 19(3):295-311.
What this means for you: Anxiety in pregnancy is very common and treatable.
⚠️ IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER
This care plan is for educational purposes only. It is NOT a substitute for professional mental health care, diagnosis, or treatment. Always work with a licensed mental health professional (psychiatrist, psychologist, therapist, social worker, or counselor) for diagnosis and personalized treatment planning.

If you are in crisis: Call or text 988 (USA Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) immediately. If you have an immediate safety emergency, call 911 (USA), 999 (UK), 000 (Australia), or 112 (Europe). You can also go to any hospital emergency room.

Mental health conditions are real medical conditions — not character flaws or weaknesses. Seeking help is a sign of strength. Recovery is possible. Most people with mental illness lead full, meaningful lives with proper treatment and support.

My Health Harbor Foundation is a nonprofit educational organization. We do not provide direct mental health care, prescribe medications, or replace your treatment team.
