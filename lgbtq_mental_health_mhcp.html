🙋👐 For Patient & Caregiver
WHO IS THIS FOR?
This plan is for veterans, active-duty service members, reservists, National Guard members, and military families. Military service creates unique mental health considerations: combat exposure, military sexual trauma (MST), traumatic brain injury (TBI), moral injury, transition stress, and identity shift. Veterans die by suicide at 1.5x the rate of non-veterans — about 17 veterans daily. Effective treatment exists. VA care is available to many; community Vet Centers offer free counseling. Asking for help is a sign of strength. This plan addresses common veteran mental health concerns and family support.

🛡️ Crisis Safety Planning
🛡️ My Crisis Safety Plan
Fill this out NOW — when you are well — so it is ready when you need it. Based on the Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention.
Veterans have elevated suicide risk. Safety plan addresses suicide risk especially with firearm access (most veteran suicides involve firearms), accessing Veterans Crisis Line, peer support, and crisis stabilization. Time-restricted firearm access during crisis saves lives.
Step 1 — My personal warning signs (thoughts, feelings, situations that come BEFORE a crisis)
Step 2 — Coping strategies I can do alone (without contacting anyone)
Step 3 — People who provide distraction (places I can go where I do not have to talk about my feelings)
Step 4 — People I can ask for help (3 trusted people with phone numbers)
Step 5 — Professionals I can contact
Step 6 — Making my environment safer (reduce access to means of harm)
Step 7 — My reasons for living (what matters most)
📌 Print this and keep it on your fridge, in your wallet, and saved on your phone. Share with your treatment team and trusted person.

🌟 Beyond the Basics — What Often Gets Missed
🌸 Trauma-Informed Approach
Understanding how past trauma affects current symptoms and healing
▼
Combat trauma, MST, training trauma, and moral injury all qualify for trauma-informed care. Many veterans have layered trauma. Trauma is treatable. Moral injury (acts done, witnessed, or failed to prevent that violate one's values) is distinct from PTSD and has specific treatment.
🕊️ Cultural & Faith Considerations
Mental health care that respects your culture, faith, and identity
▼
Military culture is its own culture. Veterans often need providers who understand military values, structure, and language. Religious/spiritual struggle common after combat — chaplains and spiritually-integrated therapy can help. Different services have different cultures — Marines vs Navy vs Army vs Air Force vs Space Force.
💵 Insurance & Financial Navigation (USA)
Getting affordable care — Medicaid, Medicare, ACA, sliding scale clinics, free options
▼
VA care is free or low-cost for many veterans. Vet Centers offer FREE counseling for combat vets, MST survivors, and families — no enrollment needed. TRICARE for active duty/dependents/some retirees. VA Caregiver benefits for family caregivers. VSOs help with applications.
🌙 Sleep & Circadian Rhythm
Why sleep matters more than almost anything else — and how to fix it
▼
Combat veterans have severe sleep issues — nightmares, hypervigilance, sleep apnea (often missed). Sleep evaluation important. Prazosin specifically helpful for combat nightmares.
🌱 Gut-Brain Connection & Nutrition
How food, microbiome, and inflammation affect mental health
▼
Many veterans have GI issues from deployment exposures (burn pits, water, etc) — register with VA. Substance use affects gut. Address proactively.
🤝 Peer Support — Why It Matters & How to Find It
Connecting with others who get it — the underrated foundation of recovery
▼
Peer support from other veterans is uniquely powerful. VFW, DAV, AmVets, IAVA, Team Red White Blue, Mission 22. Vet Centers have peer specialists. Online veteran communities. Battle buddies can be lifesaving.
📜 Psychiatric Advance Directive (PAD)
Decide your care while well — to be honored if you cannot speak for yourself
▼
Advance directives important — VA accepts. Designate decision-maker who understands military culture. Final military honors can be specified.

📚 References & Further Reading
The following peer-reviewed studies support the guidance in this care plan. Each includes a plain-English summary.
1. US Department of Veterans Affairs (2023). National Veteran Suicide Prevention Annual Report. VA.
What this means for you: 17 veterans die by suicide daily; risk-reduction strategies effective.
2. Hoge CW et al (2004). Combat duty in Iraq and Afghanistan, mental health problems. N Engl J Med, 351(1):13-22.
What this means for you: Combat exposure significantly elevates mental health conditions.
3. Steenkamp MM et al (2015). Psychotherapy for military-related PTSD. JAMA, 314(5):489-500.
What this means for you: PE and CPT have strongest evidence for combat PTSD.

What this means for you: Foundational moral injury concept; treatable with specific approaches.

⚠️ IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER
This care plan is for educational purposes only. It is NOT a substitute for professional mental health care, diagnosis, or treatment. Always work with a licensed mental health professional (psychiatrist, psychologist, therapist, social worker, or counselor) for diagnosis and personalized treatment planning.

If you are in crisis: Call or text 988 (USA Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) immediately. If you have an immediate safety emergency, call 911 (USA), 999 (UK), 000 (Australia), or 112 (Europe). You can also go to any hospital emergency room.

Mental health conditions are real medical conditions — not character flaws or weaknesses. Seeking help is a sign of strength. Recovery is possible. Most people with mental illness lead full, meaningful lives with proper treatment and support.

My Health Harbor Foundation is a nonprofit educational organization. We do not provide direct mental health care, prescribe medications, or replace your treatment team.
